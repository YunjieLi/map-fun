## The Route North to California

Spain’s push to the Pacific coast was a slow, deliberate march that began at the imperial capital of **Mexico City**. To expand northward, Spain established a massive trade and military trail that pushed through the rugged central deserts to anchor early frontier outposts like Santa Fe.

By the late 1600s, this expansion turned westward toward the Pacific coast. The colonial advance moved in two distinct stages: **Baja California** and **Alta California**.

In 1769, threatened by Russian and British expansion down the Pacific coast, Spain  established the very first permanent Spanish settlements in Alta California at **San Diego**.

The expansion to Alta California was a grueling logistical battle between a treacherous sea voyage against relentless Pacific headwinds and a punishing overland trek up the Baja peninsula. Ultimately, the land route via Tubac, AZ proved vastly superior and allowed Spain to safely drive massive herds of livestock and transport the civilian families to permanently settle in Alta California.

## The Mission-Presidio system

Along the Alta California coast, the mission and the presidio worked as a dual system for the Spanish Crown to control the frontier without massive standing armies:

- **The Missions** (21): Run by Franciscan friars, these self-sufficient religious and industrial communes were built a day's travel apart along ***El Camino Real*** ("the Royal Road"). Their goal was to convert local indigenous populations, teach them European agriculture and trades, and integrate them into Spanish society as tax-paying citizens.
- **The Presidios** (San Diego, Santa Barbara, Monterey, San Francisco): Strategically placed along the coast, these fortified military garrisons protected the missions from raids, guarded against foreign rivals, and enforced Spanish authority.

Spanish colonial rule was explicitly tailored by region. The Mission-Presidio system worked in frontier provinces like **the Californias and Texas**, whereas **New Mexico** relied on a Pueblo-Centered Civil System, governing through dense, pre-existing agricultural towns. Meanwhile, in **core Mexico**, Spain utilized large civil bureaucracies and cathedral cities built directly on top of advanced Mesoamerican state structures. In contrast, **the Caribbean and Florida** functioned as a Maritime Defensive Network, relying on heavily fortified port cities garrisoned by regular troops to protect vital shipping lanes.

## Monterey + Carmel

Within this coastal network, the neighboring sites of Monterey and Carmel were selected as the twin capitals of Spanish California:

- **Monterey - the political capital:** Chosen for its large, protected deep-water bay, Monterey became the official military and political capital of Alta California. Its presidio allowed Spanish governors to monitor incoming supply ships and defend the strategic center of the coast from foreign empires.
- **Carmel - the mission headquarters:** Located just a few miles south, Mission Carmel served as the administrative headquarters for all 21 Alta California missions. It was selected by Father Junípero Serra because the site offered fertile agricultural land, a reliable freshwater source from the Carmel River, and a safer distance from the secular military influence of the Monterey garrison.
